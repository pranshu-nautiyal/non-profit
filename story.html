A Story of Connection.

While Mithil was in middle school, his grandmother was diagnosed with Alzheimer’s. She lived in a low-resource part of Gujarat with limited access to hospitals, and much of the family was under-educated about what an Alzheimer’s diagnosis would mean.

Over the following years, through middle school and into the start of high school, Mithil visited his grandmother once every year, usually over the summer. He would talk to her, even though she was unable to speak or move, and every once in a while he would watch her let out a crackle of laughter. Every year, he showed her the art he had made, something she always looked forward to seeing, trying to ease her pain and help her stay connected, both socially and emotionally.

Mithil’s grandmother eventually progressed to a later stage of the disease, and passed away. He was left with a folder full of a year’s worth of drawings and the quiet realization that one visit a summer, however much it meant to her, could never be enough. Not for her, and not for the countless other families sitting beside a parent or grandparent who could no longer speak or move.

That realization stayed with Mithil. He didn’t want his grandmother’s story to end with a single folder of drawings seen by one person. He wanted the art, and the comfort it carried, to reach patients who had no grandson flying in each summer, and to give other young people the same chance he’d had to sit with someone, even without words, and let them know they weren’t alone.
